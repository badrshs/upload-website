How To: Your Best Printing Guide to All Your Needs.

How-To:
Printing large digital prints the right way.

So you want to print the digital photos you took with your camera, and you want to print them nice and large. Heck, maybe you want to blow them up and turn a blank wall into a mini gallery. Before you start printing, you need to make sure your images are suitable.

Here are a few things to keep in mind.

Megapixels Matter!

Just because an image looks sufficiently big onscreen doesn’t mean it’ll translate the same way on paper. If you try to print an image larger than allowed, you’ll get something that’s pixelated and unusable. It’s easy to determine the number of megapixels of an image and how large you can print it.

1. Find the dimension size.

You can find that through a program like Adobe Photoshop or the properties menu of the image file. Next, multiply the height and width, and you’ll have the answer.

2. Find the print size.

Divide the width and height dimensions by 300. Why 300? That’s the number of pixels per inch (ppi) (also a type of resolution) that’s suitable for high-quality printing. A 4608 x 3456 image will yield a 15 x 11 print. A 640 x 480 image will not be any larger than 2 x 1 inches. 

3. Making sure it is 300 ppi.

Remember to keep it at 300ppi as that is the optimised quality for print. if your image has a 72 ppi resolution, you will need to increase it with photo editing software like Photoshop under "Image" > "Image Size" > "Resolution" (make sure you deselect the “resample image” box). The lower the resolution, the larger the print size, but
quality suffers. 

4. However, do not use Image Resize to enlarge
a photo.

It’s fine to make a photo smaller or resolution higher, but you should never use this feature to make a photo larger. Doing so will only turn your photo into something heavily pixelated. 

5. Use a scanner to enlarge

If you have a flatbed scanner, you could enlarge an image by scanning it. This is ideal for enlarging old photographs or digital images that are too low-res. Before you begin the scan process, adjust your settings to scan at 600 dpi and save it as a less-compressed TIFF file. You now have a higher-resolution image that you can print larger than the original. If you are enlarging a digital image, print it out first at the highest resolution and at the maximum size on good quality paper, and then scan it.
