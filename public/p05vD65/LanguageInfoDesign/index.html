Positivity Bias

source →
A team of scientists used Big Data techniques to analyze billions of words on 10 languages (English EN, Spanish ES, French FR, German DE, Brazilian Portuguese PT, Korean KO, Simplified Chinese ZH, Russian RU, Indonesian ID and Arabic AR).
The words came from two dozen types of sources, such as books, news outlets, social media, websites, television and movie subtitles, and music lyrics. The researchers identified about 10,000 of the most commonly used words for each language, and asked native speakers to rate the happiness of these words on a scale from 1 to 9, where 9 was a smiling face, 1 was a frowning face, and 5 was neutral. They found that the most commonly used words in each language were all skewed toward the positive.

Different Language
Different Personality

Speaking two languages really could give you a split personality, researchers have found. They say that many speakers have entirely different personalities in each of the languages they speak. Linguistic difference seems to influence how speakers view events.
For example, English has a grammatical toolkit for situating actions in time that German doesn’t. As a result, German speakers tend to specify the beginnings, middles, and ends of events, but English speakers often leave out the endpoints and focus in on the action.
German DEspeakers might say, “A man leaves the house and walks to the store,” whereas an English ENspeaker would just say, “A man is walking.”

Meaning
Density

Researchers from the Université de Lyon recruited 59 male and female volunteers who were native speakers of one of seven common languages. All of them were instructed to read 20 different texts in their native languages. Any silences that lasted longer than 150 milliseconds were edited out, but the recordings were left otherwise untouched.

The more data-dense the average syllable was, the fewer of those syllables had to be spoken per second — and thus the slower the speech.
